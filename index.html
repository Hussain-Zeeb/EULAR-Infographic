Recognising Still’s Disease as a single condition

A Unified Perspective on Still’s Disease

Systemic juvenile idiopathic arthritis (sJIA) and adult-onset Still’s disease (AOSD) should be recognised as a single condition, Still’s disease, and not seen as separate disorders.1 This is the key recommendation from the 2024 EULAR/PReS consensus report on the diagnosis and management of Still’s disease. 

Understanding that Still’s disease is a single disease entity is key to addressing the shared symptoms, diagnostic challenges, and treatment strategies for patients of all ages. Explore how these guidelines can help to standardise care and may enhance outcomes for everyone living with Still’s disease.1

Diagnosis Without Arthritis Requirement

Breaking Barriers in Diagnosis

Delays in diagnosing Still's disease can lead to serious complications and chronic persistent disease. EULAR/PReS states that while arthralgia is a common presentation in Still’s disease, arthritis often appears later: removing arthritis from the criteria to make a diagnosis avoids unnecessary and potentially detrimental delays.1

Early Use of Biologics

Prioritising Biologics for Early Intervention

Why Early Biologics Matter

EULAR/PReS highlights the importance of early treatment with IL-1 and IL-6 inhibitors to control inflammation and prevent complications. No formal randomised controlled trials have addressed the timing of initiation of IL-1 or IL-6 inhibitors in Still’s disease.2

Recommendations

Once a patient, over the age of 8 months and 10 kg has been diagnosed with Still’s disease, with active systemic features of moderate to high disease activity, EULAR/PReS recommends IL-1 inhibitors and IL-6 inhibitors as the first-line options for biologic therapy.1

Adhering to UK Guidance While Aligning with EULAR/PReS:
At the time the UK-specific NICE guidance was published, AOSD and sJIA were considered separate conditions. While the EULAR/PReS guidelines advocate early biologic use,1 UK healthcare professionals are expected to follow NICE guidance* and offer initial treatment with non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs), glucocorticoids, or conventional DMARDs before progressing to biologics.2 This approach ensures adherence to national policies while leveraging the efficacy of biologic therapies at appropriate stages.

*NICE recommendations do not override the responsibility of healthcare professionals to make decisions appropriate to the individual patient.2

Monitoring Complications

Proactive Monitoring for Complications

Severe or life-threatening complications can develop at any point during the course of Still’s disease. Patients should be actively screened and monitored for:1

Macrophage Activation Syndrome (MAS)

A hyperinflammatory state requiring

  • high-dose glucocorticoids and
  • immunomodulators.

Still’s Lung Disease

Characterised by interstitial inflammation, often associated with younger patients and recurrent MAS episodes. Early screening through

  • pulse oximetry
  • CT scans
  • and pulmonary function tests is critical.

The EULAR/PReS guidelines recommend collaboration with expert centres for managing these complications and ensuring patients receive tailored, evidence-based care.1
